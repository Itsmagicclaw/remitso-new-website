Money Transmitter License USA: Complete
2026 State-by-State Guide

Navigating the complex landscape of money transmitter licenses in the USA is crucial for fintech startups. This guide provides a state-by-state breakdown of the requirements, costs, and timelines for obtaining a money transmitter license in 2026.

⏱ 12 min read · 🖊 Satish Shrivastava · 📅 2026

Launching a money transfer business in the United States requires navigating a complex regulatory landscape. Unlike most industries, remittance operators must satisfy both federal requirements from the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) and state-level money transmitter licensing (MTL) demands. This 2026 guide walks you through every requirement, fee structure, timeline, and compliance obligation across all 50 states.

Quick Answer
  • A money transmitter license (MTL) is required in 49 US states to legally send or receive money on behalf of others.
  • Montana exempts most money transmission activities from state licensing.
  • Federal FinCEN registration as an MSB (Money Services Business) is mandatory and separate from state MTLs.
  • Licensing fees and surety bond requirements vary by state, ranging from $250 (Montana) to $500,000+ (California).
  • Complete licensing timeline: 3–18 months depending on state and application complexity.
  • Ongoing compliance includes AML/CFT program management, SAR filing, and transaction monitoring.
⚠ Regulatory Disclaimer: This guide provides operational information only and does not constitute legal or compliance advice. Licensing requirements change frequently and vary by jurisdiction. Consult a licensed attorney or compliance specialist before launching your money transfer business.

What Is a Money Transmitter License in the USA?

A money transmitter license is a regulatory authorization that permits a business to send, receive, or exchange funds on behalf of customers. In the United States, "money transmission" is broadly defined as accepting currency or monetary value from a customer, holding it, and then paying it to a third party (either domestically or internationally). Without a valid MTL, conducting such activities is illegal and can result in criminal prosecution, civil fines, and asset seizure.

The regulatory framework originated decades ago to prevent fraud and money laundering. Today, 49 states require explicit MTL licensing. Montana is the sole state with a significant exemption: money transmission activities there are exempt if the business does not accept or solicit money from the public via networks or agents. For practical purposes, nearly all U.S. operators need state licensing.

The licensing process is not a simple administrative filing. States require extensive application materials, background checks, financial statements, compliance documentation, and in many cases, a substantial surety bond. The goal is to ensure only qualified, well-capitalized, and trustworthy entities operate in the remittance space.

Federal vs. State Licensing: What's the Difference?

The U.S. uses a dual regulatory model: federal oversight through FinCEN and state oversight through state financial regulators. These are separate systems, and both are required.

Federal registration with FinCEN is streamlined: free to register, $300 to renew every two years. State licensing is the complex, time-consuming, and expensive component. Each state defines its own requirements, application procedures, fees, and ongoing compliance obligations. This is why launching a U.S. money transfer business requires strategic planning around which states to target first.

FinCEN MSB Registration Requirements

FinCEN is the U.S. Treasury Department's anti-money laundering authority. All money services businesses operating in the United States must register with FinCEN as an MSB (Money Services Business). This includes money transmitters, check cashiers, bureau de changes, and related entities.

03

File Form 107 through FinCEN's online portal at fincen.gov (free registration, no fee).
04
Immediate Activation
Registration is active immediately upon filing. You receive a registration number to show compliance.
05
Biennial Renewal

Figure 2: FinCEN MSB registration is a simple five-step process with free initial filing and $300 biennial renewal.

As of December 2025, approximately 29,394 MSBs were registered with FinCEN. Registration is straightforward: file Form 107 online, receive a registration number immediately, and pay $300 every two years. However, missing the 180-day deadline is a federal violation and can trigger criminal liability.

State-by-State MTL Requirements

Each of the 49 states (except Montana) maintains its own money transmitter licensing regime. Key state regulators include the state Department of Financial Regulation, Superintendent of Banks, or Division of Banking. Below is a detailed breakdown of major U.S. markets and their requirements.

California (DBO/DFPI)

California has the strictest regime in the U.S. The state requires a Department of Financial Protection and Innovation (DFPI) license. Application fees start at $5,000, and surety bonds range from $250,000 to $7 million depending on transaction volume. The process typically takes 12–18 months and requires extensive documentation, background investigations, and ongoing compliance reporting. Many startups avoid California initially due to the regulatory burden.

Texas (TDOB)

Texas is one of the more entrepreneur-friendly states. The Texas Department of Banking (TDOB) processes applications in 3–6 months with licensing fees of $1,000–$2,000 and surety bonds typically $25,000–$500,000. Texas-licensed operators often expand nationwide once they have one state established.

Florida (OFR)

Florida's Office of Financial Regulation (OFR) license costs $250–$500 in fees and requires a surety bond of $50,000–$300,000. Application processing takes 4–8 months. Florida's reasonable timelines and moderate costs make it attractive for startups.

New York (DFS)

New York requires a DFS (Department of Financial Services) license separate from the general MTL. New York also has the BitLicense framework for virtual asset businesses. MTL fees are $1,000–$5,000, surety bonds $100,000–$500,000, but processing can take 12–24 months. Compliance expectations are rigorous.

Illinois (Secretary of State)

Illinois allows MTL application through the Secretary of State. License fees are modest ($250–$1,000), surety bonds typically $25,000–$100,000, and processing time is 4–6 months. Illinois is a reasonable starting point for Midwest expansion.

Washington (DFI)

Washington's Department of Financial Institutions (DFI) processes MTL applications in 3–6 months. License fees start at $250–$1,000, with surety bonds from $25,000–$500,000. Washington is competitive and fairly accessible.

Montana (No State License Required)

Montana is the sole state with no MTL requirement. However, federal FinCEN registration is still mandatory. Many operators use Montana as a legal domicile for this reason, though they still must comply with states where their customers are located.

Surety Bond and Net Worth Requirements

Surety bonds protect customers if your business fails or engages in misconduct. Most states require bonds ranging from $25,000 to $500,000, with California requiring up to $7 million for high-volume operators. Bonds typically cost 1–3% of the bond amount annually.

Surety Bond Requirements and Cost Factors
Transaction Volume Scaling
Bond amounts increase with your projected annual transaction volume. A $1M annual volume typically requires $50K–$100K bond; $10M+ volume requires $250K–$500K or higher. Surety companies assess your revenue and risk profile.
Annual Cost
Expect to pay 1–3% of the bond amount per year as a surety premium. A $100K bond costs roughly $1,000–$3,000 annually. Surety companies assess credit score, business financials, and compliance track record when pricing premiums.
Net Worth Minimums
Many states (California, New York, Texas) require minimum net worth of $50K–$500K depending on transaction volume. You must demonstrate adequate capitalization to handle customer funds safely and cover operational losses if needed.
Capital Reserves
Regulators scrutinize your balance sheet to ensure you can absorb fraud losses, chargebacks, and operational setbacks. Maintain healthy cash reserves and avoid carrying excessive debt relative to equity.

Figure 5: Surety bonds scale with transaction volume and require 1–3% annual premiums; net worth minimums ensure capitalization.

Net worth and capital reserve requirements are state-specific but typically tie to your projected transaction volume. A startup planning $1M annual transactions might need $50K–$100K net worth; a $100M+ operation requires $250K–$1M+. These requirements ensure you can absorb operational losses and protect customer funds.

Ongoing Compliance Obligations After Licensing

Obtaining an MTL is the beginning, not the end. Licensed operators face continuous compliance obligations under the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA), FinCEN regulations, and state requirements.

  • AML/CFT Program: Establish a formal Anti-Money Laundering and Countering the Financing of Terrorism program. Designate a compliance officer, create written policies, conduct training, and maintain documentation.
  • Know Your Customer (KYC): Collect and verify customer identity information at onboarding. Risk-based approach: standard KYC for low-risk customers, Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) for high-risk profiles.
  • Transaction Monitoring: Monitor customer transactions for suspicious patterns. Flag structuring, rapid high-volume transfers to high-risk jurisdictions, and other red flags.
  • Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs): File a SAR with FinCEN if you detect activity indicating money laundering, terrorist financing, or other crimes. Deadline: 30 days after detection.
  • Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs): Report cash transactions over $10,000 to FinCEN via the Currency Transaction Report system. Automated for most payment processors.
  • Travel Rule Compliance: For cross-border transactions, transmit originating and beneficiary customer information to receiving institutions (FATF Recommendation 16). Timelines vary by corridor.
  • Sanctions Screening: Screen all customers and transactions against OFAC sanctions lists, UN lists, and other government blacklists. Real-time, automated screening is essential.
  • Regulatory Reporting: File annual financial reports, compliance certifications, and other filings mandated by your state regulator.
  • Audit Trail and Record-Keeping: Maintain detailed records of all transactions, customer files, and compliance activities for a minimum of 5 years.

Non-compliance with these obligations can result in fines up to $100,000+ per violation, license revocation, criminal charges, and reputational damage. Many startups underestimate the operational and technical cost of staying compliant.
