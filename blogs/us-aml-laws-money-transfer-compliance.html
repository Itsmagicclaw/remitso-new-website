Key AML Laws in the US and Their Impact on Money Transfer Services

Anti-Money Laundering (AML) laws are a cornerstone of financial security in the United States. For money transfer service providers, banks, fintech companies, and other financial institutions, AML compliance is not just regulatory—it is fundamental to building trust, preventing financial crime, and safeguarding global remittance networks.

Money laundering is a serious crime, often linked to fraud, drug trafficking, cybercrime, human trafficking, and terrorist financing. By enforcing AML laws, the US government equips financial institutions to detect suspicious activity, prevent illicit funds from circulating, and protect the global financial system.

The Foundation: Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) of 1970

The Bank Secrecy Act (BSA), also known as the Currency and Foreign Transactions Reporting Act, was the first major federal law aimed at combating money laundering. It requires financial institutions to:

  • Maintain detailed records of financial transactions
  • Report transactions exceeding $10,000 via Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs)
  • Detect and report suspicious activity, including unusual or structured transactions

For money transfer service companies, compliance with the BSA ensures that their operations are legally sound and capable of detecting fraudulent or suspicious transactions.

Other Key AML Laws Affecting US Money Transfer Services

Money Laundering Control Act of 1986

This act criminalized money laundering in the US and allowed law enforcement to seize funds connected to illicit activity. For money transfer companies, this emphasizes the importance of tracking and documenting every transaction.

Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1988

This law expanded the definition of financial institutions and required verification for high-value transactions. Money transfer services handling cross-border remittances must comply with these verification requirements.

Annunzio-Wylie Anti-Money Laundering Act of 1992

This act reinforced reporting requirements, introduced Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs), and allowed regulators to revoke licenses for violations. Companies must implement robust internal monitoring and reporting systems.

Money Laundering Suppression Act of 1994

This Act required money services businesses (MSBs), including remittance companies, to register with FinCEN and maintain compliance programs. It also mandated ongoing staff training and stricter reporting obligations.

USA PATRIOT Act of 2001

After 9/11, compliance expanded to include terrorist financing. The PATRIOT Act requires:

  • Customer Identification Procedures (CIP)
  • Customer Due Diligence (CDD)
  • Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) for high-risk accounts

Money transfer companies must verify identities, screen customers against watchlists, and monitor international transfers for potential terrorist activity.

Anti-Money Laundering Act of 2020 (AMLA)

The AMLA modernized AML regulations and introduced the Corporate Transparency Act (CTA), requiring money transfer companies to:

  • Identify beneficial owners of corporate clients
  • Implement systems for cryptocurrency transactions
  • Strengthen reporting and whistleblower protections

Modern remittance companies must comply with AMLA to operate legally, prevent misuse of funds, and protect customer trust.

Core AML Requirements for Money Transfer Services

To remain compliant in the US, money transfer companies must implement comprehensive AML programs, typically built on these five pillars:

1. Designate a Compliance Officer

A dedicated AML compliance officer oversees regulatory adherence, internal policies, staff training, and reporting procedures.

2. Develop Internal Policies

Companies must maintain formal policies covering customer verification, risk assessment, transaction monitoring, and reporting processes.

3. Train Employees

All employees must understand AML regulations, KYC rules, and how to detect suspicious transactions.

4. Audit and Test Programs

Regular audits ensure AML systems are effective and allow companies to identify and correct compliance gaps early.

5. Implement Risk-Based Customer Due Diligence

Financial institutions assess risks associated with customers and transactions, including:

  • Know Your Customer (KYC): Verifying identity, address, and source of funds
  • Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD): For high-risk or international customers
  • Transaction Monitoring: Detecting unusual or high-value activity

These procedures help money transfer companies avoid fines and protect their reputation.

How AML Compliance Impacts Money Transfer Operations

Safer Global Transactions

AML compliance ensures that all funds passing through money transfer systems are legitimate and traceable.

Regulatory Approval and Licensing

Non-compliant companies risk losing their MSB license, which is legally required to operate in the US.

Customer Trust

Customers choose services that guarantee safety, transparency, and compliance — especially for international transfers.

Integration with Fintech Solutions

Modern money transfer companies integrate AML software, KYC verification tools, and automated monitoring systems to streamline compliance.

International Standards and Collaboration

The US follows global AML standards from the Financial Action Task Force (FATF), which influence:

  • Risk-based transaction monitoring
  • Customer due diligence procedures
  • Cross-border compliance and data sharing

Adopting FATF-aligned processes helps companies operate legally on a global scale.

Compliance Challenges for Money Transfer Companies

  • Rapid customer growth makes monitoring more complex
  • High transaction volume requires automated detection systems
  • Crypto transactions introduce new AML risks
  • Regulations change frequently and require constant updates

Solution: Implement strong AML software, continuous training, and automated real-time monitoring.

Tips for Effective AML Compliance in Money Transfer Services

  • Use no-code AML tools for easy deployment
  • Automate customer verification and monitoring
  • Maintain real-time reports for regulators
  • Conduct frequent risk assessments and audits
  • Train employees on new risks, including digital asset fraud

Fintech remittance companies use these methods to scale globally while staying compliant.
