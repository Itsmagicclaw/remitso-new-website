Understanding Open Banking in the Context of Remittances

Open banking refers to the practice of allowing third-party providers secure access to bank data and payment infrastructure through application programming interfaces (APIs). This model enables financial services to innovate faster, reduce friction in payments, and enhance compliance.

Globally, open banking adoption has been driven by regulations such as:

  • PSD2 (Payment Services Directive 2) in the European Union
  • UK Open Banking Standard
  • MAS API Frameworks in Singapore
  • FinCEN guidelines for transparent financial data sharing

In remittances, open banking allows providers to authenticate accounts, verify ownership, and process payments directly between banks, reducing dependency on intermediaries and minimizing settlement delays. For consumers, this translates into faster, lower-cost, and more secure cross-border transfers.

The Rising Importance of Remittances in the Global Economy

Remittances are more than personal transfers—they form a backbone of economic support for many countries. In 2024, global transaction values for cross-border remittances are estimated at $3.9 trillion, increasing to $6.5 trillion by 2028.

Key trends include:

  • High-volume corridors: U.S.–India, Europe–Africa, Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC)–Asia
  • Country-specific growth: Germany and France are leading remittance senders in the EU
  • Social impact: Supporting families, education, and local businesses in developing countries

However, remittance providers face five persistent challenges:

  • Customer experience: Consumers demand instant, intuitive mobile payments.
  • Competition: Low fees and speed are now minimum requirements.
  • Business complexity: Multi-currency, multi-jurisdiction operations require nuanced solutions.
  • Fraud and compliance: Varying AML, KYC, and regulatory standards increase operational risk.
  • Settlement delays: Instant settlement is still not globally available, causing friction and uncertainty.

Open banking offers practical solutions to address these challenges while keeping providers compliant with international standards such as FATF recommendations, AMLD6, and local regulatory authorities like FCA and MAS.

Six Ways Open Banking Enhances Remittance Services

1. Enabling Faster and Cheaper Transfers

Traditional remittances often rely on multi-step bank transfers, intermediaries, and legacy systems like SWIFT, which introduce delays and increase costs. Open banking facilitates account-to-account (A2A) transfers via APIs, enabling near-instant settlements.

  • Reduced costs: Fewer intermediaries mean lower fees.
  • Faster transfers: Direct bank-to-bank payments speed up delivery times.
  • Operational efficiency: Providers can automate reconciliation and reduce manual errors.

2. Improving Security and Fraud Prevention

Open banking enables robust fraud prevention through real-time verification of accounts and transaction patterns. Providers can access:

  • Verified bank account details
  • Transaction histories and balance data
  • Identity confirmation through KYC/KYB APIs

3. Simplifying Customer Authentication and UX

Strong Customer Authentication (SCA) often requires multiple verification steps, causing friction for users. Open banking streamlines authentication by:

  • Reducing redundant verification for returning users
  • Allowing seamless login via bank credentials
  • Integrating identity checks directly into payment flows

4. Powering Smarter Compliance and AML Monitoring

Remittance providers operate in highly regulated environments. Open banking enables real-time KYC and KYB validation, helping providers:

  • Verify sender and recipient identities
  • Assess risk profiles instantly
  • Ensure adherence to AML, CFT, and sanctions regulations

5. Supporting Financial Inclusion and Transparency

Open banking opens access for underbanked and underserved populations, offering secure digital payment options without requiring traditional accounts.

  • Transparent fees with no hidden costs
  • Easier cross-border transfers to rural or remote areas
  • Increased trust through regulated, secure data flows

6. Enabling Innovation Through Embedded Finance

Embedded finance allows fintechs and remittance providers to integrate payments directly into digital services like:

  • Payroll systems
  • Mobile wallets
  • E-commerce platforms

Through open banking APIs, providers can create white-label solutions that combine payments, verification, and compliance in one seamless workflow. This enables businesses to innovate rapidly and meet evolving consumer expectations.

The Future of Open Banking in Remittance Innovation

Open banking adoption is accelerating globally, supported by:

  • ISO 20022 standards for unified payments messaging
  • Central bank instant payment systems
  • Partnerships between fintechs, neobanks, and technology enablers

The future promises:

  • Full interoperability across borders
  • Universal access to digital wallets and bank accounts
  • Reduced fraud and enhanced transparency

Conclusion — A More Connected, Transparent, and Efficient Future

Open banking is reshaping the remittance landscape by addressing long-standing pain points in speed, security, compliance, and customer experience. For providers, it offers operational efficiencies, lower costs, and enhanced compliance with global regulations. For consumers, it ensures fast, transparent, and secure cross-border payments.

As open banking matures and regulatory frameworks evolve, providers that adopt API-driven, compliant, and customer-centric solutions will be best positioned to capture the growth in the global remittance market, projected to reach $6.5 trillion by 2028.
